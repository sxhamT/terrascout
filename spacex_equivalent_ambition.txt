To pull off a SpaceX-style booster landing in a 10x10m GPS-denied environment, you need to move beyond "seeing" shapes and start "measuring" physics. Since you are descending from 15m, your sensor choice determines whether your drone sees a "flat grey box" or a "slanted, dusty concrete slab."

Here is the breakdown of the two technologies and how to configure them for your 5.1 implementation.
1. Synthetic vs. RTX Lidar: The Technical Deep Dive
Synthetic Lidar (The Legacy Path)

Synthetic Lidar in Isaac Sim is essentially a Post-Processing Effect. It takes the Depth Buffer (Z-buffer) from a camera and mathematically transforms those pixel depths into a point cloud.

    How it works: It casts rays based on a pixel grid. If a pixel is at (u,v) with depth d, it calculates the (x,y,z) coordinate.

    The "SpaceX" Problem: Because it's based on a camera view, it doesn't understand materials. If your landing spot is a shiny metal plate (like a booster deck) or a wet puddle in Bhopal, synthetic lidar will give you perfect, clean data. In the real world, light would bounce off or scatter.

    Performance: It’s CPU-heavy when converting the buffer to a point cloud, which causes "stuttering" in your control loop—dangerous for a landing drone.

RTX Lidar (The Modern/Physical Path)

RTX Lidar uses the RTX (Ray Tracing Texel) hardware to simulate actual light transport.

    How it works: It fires "beams" that have divergence (they get wider over distance). It calculates the Intensity (return strength) based on the material's reflectivity defined in your USD.

    The "SpaceX" Advantage: It simulates "Multi-return." If a beam hits the edge of a rough rock on your heightmap, part of the beam hits the rock and part hits the ground. RTX Lidar can return both points. This is critical for detecting if a landing spot is truly clear or just "mostly" flat.

    Precision: It operates entirely on the GPU. For your 10x10m area, you can have 100,000 points per second without dropping below 60 FPS.

3. Sensor Configuration: Scan Angles & Resolution

For a 10x10m area from 15m height, a "spinning" Lidar is standard, but for a landing drone, a Downward-Facing Cone (like a Velodyne or Ouster) is better.
Scan Angles (Field of View)

    Horizontal FOV: 360∘. You need to see if a side-gust is pushing you toward an obstacle outside the 10x10m zone.

    Vertical FOV: +15∘ to −90∘.

        The −90∘ (Nadir) is critical—it’s looking straight down at the landing feet.

        The +15∘ allows you to see the "horizon" of the 10x10m area as you descend, helping with orientation.

Angular Resolution

    Vertical Resolution: 0.5∘ to 1.0∘. At 15m, a 1∘ resolution gives you a point every 26 cm. This is too sparse to find your 1m "smooth" spot reliably.

    The Fix: Use an RTX Lidar profile with 128 channels (like a Velodyne VLS-128). This will give you sub-5cm resolution at 15m, allowing your code to "see" the roughness of the heightmap clearly.

4. Implementation Strategy (GPS-Denied)

Since you can't use GPS, your drone is "blind" to its absolute position. It only knows where it is relative to the points it sees.

    Lidar Odometry: Use the Isaac Compute Odometry node in your Action Graph (or code). It compares the current point cloud to the previous one to calculate how much the drone has moved.

    Point Cloud Filtering: Because the drone is 0.6m, filter out any points that are further than 5m away when you are below 5m altitude. This focuses the "Roughness Check" (that std dev math we discussed) on the immediate landing footprint.

    The "Slope" Logic: At 15m, your slope detection will be "fuzzy." As you hit 5m, your code should switch to a High Precision Mode, where it specifically looks for the 4 contact points of your landing gear.

Summary of the 5.1 Code Setup

    Trigger: on_playback_tick to ensure the RTX buffer is ready.

    Validation: Use the roughness < 0.03m threshold to reject the heightmap's jagged edges.

The "SpaceX" Flight Profile (Trajectory)

To mimic a Falcon 9 booster, you should split your logic into three distinct "state machine" phases:
Phase A: Topography Patrol (15m–30m)

    Goal: Map the 10x10m area.

    Speed: 1–2 m/s in a "Lawnmower" or "Circular" pattern.

    Logic: Your code should store the Lidar points in a Point Cloud Map. Once the map has high enough density (e.g., 500 points per m2), run your "Landing Spot Finder" script to pick the (x,y) coordinate with the lowest slope and lowest Z-variance.

Phase B: Guidance to "Hover Point" (MPC)

    Goal: Move from the current position to directly above the (x,y) target.

    Method: Use Model Predictive Control (MPC). MPC is better here because it can handle "constraints" (e.g., "don't tilt the drone more than 20∘" or "don't exceed 3 m/s").

    SpaceX Feel: MPC allows for that smooth, curved trajectory where the drone "leans" into the movement and cancels its momentum perfectly as it reaches the hover point.

Phase C: Final Descent (PID)

    Goal: Vertical touchdown.

    Method: Switch to a PID Controller. Once you are at xtarget​,ytarget​, you only care about maintaining a zero-velocity horizontal position and a constant vertical descent rate.

    Logic: The PID is more "reactive" and faster than MPC for handling the sudden "ground effect" (turbulence simulated in Isaac Sim) as the drone gets within 1m of the mesh.

3. Handling the "GPS-Denied" Factor

Since you don't have GPS, your drone's "internal clock" for position will drift.

    Odometry Stability: At 30m, if your heightmap is very "repetitive," the Lidar Odometry might slip. 15m is safer for odometry because the sensor "sees" the unique textures and jagged edges of your rough surface more clearly, giving it "anchors" to stay positioned.

    Relative Target: Don't tell the drone "Go to (5,5)." Tell it "Go to X+2,Y−1 relative to the spot identified by the Lidar."
